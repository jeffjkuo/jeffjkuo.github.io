Hello there, welcome to my site!

I am an Economics Ph.D. candidate at George Washington University. I received an M.A. Economics degree from the Maxwell School of Citizenship and Public Affairs at Syracuse University and an honorable BA-MS joint degree in International Business and Trade from National Chengchi University. Before heading to the U.S. for graduate study, I was a research assistant at Institute of Economics, Academia Sinica, responsible for assisting a policy research sponsored by Ministry of Science and Technology in Taiwan. The project focused on revisiting the decennial stagflation and wage rigidity in Taiwan's labor market after Millenium. We found there was a public misunderstanding of the timing of the economic downturn. Furthermore, after autotomizing compensation trends across the different age groups, we argued that the reform of higher education in Taiwan was the scapegoat of slow-adjusted trade and FDI policies under the multilateralism regime, not the leading cause of stagflation.

My primary research interests encompass International Economics, Political Economy, Macroeconomics. For example, how do economic activities in an open economy, such as trade in goods and services, international capital flows, foreign direct investment, and regional trade agreements, affect the welfare of the nations, citizens, firms, and policies cooperation between the countries? I am currently focusing on open-border policies in Taiwan. I want to know how the exposure of the long-time restricted visitors from China causes the realignment of political ideology and switch party recognitions across municipalities.

The research motivation is partially due to my background and beliefs of politics and economics are inseparable.

I witnessed the economic miracle along with the wave of globalization growing up in Taiwan. The economic growth soon triggered the process of democratization and transformed the authoritarian regime into a liberal society. Also, the frequency and magnitude of the political movements seem increasing among Asian emerging markets, which demonstrates another example of the synergy between trade and politics. The regional integration was praised for helping developing countries, but they also embark on social inequality and backlashes. Hence, identifying the mechanism empirically by constructing the models to test the country's trade policies and solving the political-economic equilibria has become the core of my research interests.

Besides being a Ph.D. student, I am a Lecturer, Economist, and Graduate Teaching Assistant at the GW Economics Department. During the regular semesters, I am responsible for coordinating and teaching the discussion sessions of the M.A. Applied Economics Program. By constantly serving as a teaching assistant of the master-level courses, I am good at consulting the empirical models built to solve problems in the real world. Furthermore, besides being trained using various statistical packages like Stata, R, and SAS, I also have a deep understanding of mathematical analysis and classical economic theory. I have assisted in teaching undergrauate-level Econometrics and Macroeconomics. After being named the principal teaching assistant of the M.A. Applied Economics program, I help graduate-level Probability and Statistics, Econometrics, Microeconometrics, and Time Series Analysis. In Summer 2020, I taught an online graduate-level Survey of International Economics (syllabus) via the Blackbaord Ultra.

I participate in all of the academic seminars and workshops held in GW Economics. I am a member of H. O. Stekler Research Program on Forecasting and Student Association for Graduate Economists (SAGE). Outside GW, I hold student memberships of American Economics Associations (AEA), International Trade and Finance Association (IT&FA), National Association of Business Economics (NABE), The National Society of Leadership and Success (NSLS), and Southern Economics Association (SEA).
