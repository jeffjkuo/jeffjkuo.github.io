Hello there, welcome to my site!

I am an Economics Ph.D. candidate at George Washington University. I received an M.A. degree in Economics from the Maxwell School of Citizenship and Public Affairs at Syracuse University and an honorable joint BA-MS degree in International Business and Trade from National Chengchi University. Before heading to the U.S. for graduate study, I was a research assistant at the Institute of Economics, Academia Sinica, responsible for assisting the Ministry of Science and Technology-sponsored research. The project focused on the decennial stagflation and wage rigidity in modern Taiwan's labor market, which some argue was due to the systematic reformation of education.

My primary research interests encompass International Economics, Political Economy, Macroeconomics. For example, how do open-market activities, such as trade in goods and services, capital flows, FDI, RTA affect the welfare of the nations, citizens and the international cooperations? Moreover, by constantly serving as a teaching assistant of graduate-level econometrics courses, I am good at building empirical models to solve economics problems using various statistical packages, such as Stata, R, and SAS.

My current research project focuses on how open-border policies toward the long-time restricted area visitors realign political ideology and switch party recognitions. Growing up in modern Taiwan, I have witnessed the economic miracle and understood how imperative globalization, trade, and FDI play parts in transforming the economy. The openness of an economy, economic growth, and political reformation are intertwined, as we could easily see from the increasing frequency and the magnitude of the political movements among Asian Tigers after decades of economic booming. On the other hand, multilateralism progressed as more countries grasp the fruit of trade. The regional integration opens the gate for the closed countries to cooperate; however, it might also embark social inequality problems and backlashed the government. Hence, I believe economics and politics are inseparable and closely linked. Identifying the mechanism behind international trade's evolution and solving the political-economic equilibria by constructing theoretical models and applying empirics has become the core of my research interests.

Besides being a Ph.D. student, I am a Lecturer, Economist, and Graduate Teaching Assistant at the GW Economics Department. During the regular semesters, I am responsible for coordinating and teaching the discussion sessions of the M.A. Applied Economics Program. The survey courses I have assisted in teaching are Econometrics and Macroeconomics. After being assigned as the principal teaching assistant of the M.A. Applied Economics Program, I help graduate-level Probability and Statistics, Econometrics, Microeconometrics, and Time Series Analysis. In Summer 2020, I taught a graduate-level Survey of International Economics under the virtual environment on Blackboard Collaborate Ultra. At GW, I am a student member of the H. O. Stekler Research Program on Forecasting and the Student Association for Graduate Economists (SAGE). Outside GW, I hold student memberships of American Economics Associations (AEA), International Trade and Finance Association (IT&FA), National Association of Business Economics (NABE), The National Society of Leadership and Success (NSLS), and Southern Economics Association (SEA).
